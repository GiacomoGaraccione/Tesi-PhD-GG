\chapter{Intelligent Agents for Software Modeling Tasks}

The three empirical studies described in the previous chapters highlighted the strength of gamification as a motivator for student engagement, as well as an effective tool to support academic growth by increasing diagram quality and grades. The limitations tied to the two tool's evaluation systems, however, reduced their potential benefits, pointing to the need to find a way to extend the set of available solutions beyond the scope of synonyms and structures that can be identified by a single human. 

Additionally, the diffusion of large language models was becoming more and more common during the 2023-2024 academic year, showing several benefits and applications in the software engineering domain such as code generation and testing. Since the use of LLMs for model based engineering activities was still a relatively unexplored area in Autumn 2023, a new research direction was undertaken, with the goal of identifying ways in which intelligent agents could be used to support MBE activities such as diagram generation or evaluation.

For this purpose, two different research projects began in Autumn 2023, first as Master theses and then as complete studies, with the goal of observing an LLM's ability to generate valid diagram content, aiming to observe whether significant differences existed between the LLM-generated artifacts and student-produced diagrams: exploring this research area would provide insights on how agentic tools could be integrated in the teaching process and also derive guidelines for students on how to make better use of such tools. These two studies, focused respectively on UML class diagrams and UML use case diagrams were presented in \cite{debari2024evaluating} and \cite{garaccione2025vega}.

Following the results of these two studies, a third one was launched to implement a new software that could enhance the solution definition process for class diagrams by introducing LLM support: the architecture was created to be compatible with the Apollon library and create solution drafts starting from textual descriptions, both as editable diagrams and as data structures to be used with the evaluation engine. A preliminary proof of concept analysis was performed with two exercises, comparing the produced output diagrams with the reference solutions of existing exercises, identifying the limits of the architecture and allowing for the definition of next steps that guide the research activities to be performed after the end of the PhD. The new assistant was described in \cite{garaccione2026automated}.

Lastly, the findings from the first study led to subsequent theses that went beyond the original goal: instead of comparing human-made diagrams with those generated by a single architecture, new works were defined to compare multiple architectures on the same sets of requirements, with the goal of defining effective prompting strategies to enhance the solution-creating tool. One of these theses was then converted into a research article, and presented in \cite{garaccione2025models}.

\section{Study 1: Evaluating Automatically Generated UML class diagrams}
%Study 1: Evaluating LLMs on UML Class Diagram Exercises

The first study started as a thesis in Autumn 2023, and had the goal of comparing an LLM's ability to generate class diagrams in comparison to a student. The goal for the study was to observe whether there were certain areas where an agent could perform similar to human solvers, or potentially even areas where it could outperform them. A controlled experiment was conducted using the data obtained with the thesis work to evaluate the LLM's ability against the human solver, with the design being formulated using the Goal-Question-Metric template according to Table \ref{tab:llm4mod1_gqm}, expressing the goal as \textit{analyzing the use of LLM agents for the purpose of comparing the quality and correctness of generated UML Class Diagrams from natural language requirements, from the point of view of Software Modeling instructors}.

\subsubsection{Study Design}
The first step to conduct the study was to collect exercises to compare LLM-generated and student-produced diagrams with; exercises were selected from various online sources, with the caveat that they had to provide a natural language description written in either English or Italian, and they had to be accompanied by a class diagram showing the intended reference solution. A total of 20 exercises were retrieved, covering several application domains and with a range of difficulties, allowing for multiple levels of comparison. 

Certain constraints were defined before conducting the study on both the student side and the LLM side, to ensure all exercises would be solved with the same conditions. For what concerns the student side, all 20 exercises were solved by the student for their thesis work with constraints set to simulate a real-life exam setting: a maximum of 30 minutes could be spent on solving each exercise, with neither any interaction with other people nor the use of any external sources such as textbooks, notes, or internet queries allowed. Diagrams were produced with the Microsoft Visio modeling tool-

Regarding the LLM, the model selected was OpenAI's ChatGPT-4, one of the best performing models at the time the study was conducted in Autumn 2023. No specific prompt engineering strategy was adopted, but rather, a simple prompt stating simply the goal and the format was employed:  

\begin{quote}
\textit{``Create a UML Class Diagram of the given exercise and give me the
PlantUML code''}, followed by the text of the exercise.
\end{quote}

The generated PlantUML code was then rendered graphically to generate image files, which were then used for the comparison with the corresponding student diagrams.

\subsection{Research Questions and Metrics}

The experiment was structured around two research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{RQ1:}] Does the use of LLM agents have an impact on the quality and correctness of UML Class Diagrams generated from natural language requirements?
    \item[\textbf{RQ2:}] What aspects of the natural language requirements have an impact on the quality of the generated UML Class Diagrams?
\end{itemize}

Diagram quality was measured according to the three dimensions identified by the framework by Bolloju and Leung \cite{bolloju2006assisting}, where, for each dimension, the total count of corresponding errors was computed. This meant identifying syntax errors such as the absence of multiplicity values or names in associations, the absence of attribute types, or the use of incorrect UML symbols, semantic errors such as incorrect values for a given association, the use of incorrect relationship types, incorrect attribute or method placement, or methods that could not be derived from relationships and attributes; lastly, pragmatic errors included redundant attributes or associations, specialization relationships with no way to distinguish subclasses, or style and layout inconsistencies in the graphical representation of the diagram. These three metrics can be computed through visual assessment of the diagrams, and syntax and pragmatic error counts can be computed without any comparison with reference solution, since they refer to intrinsic quality conditions that are applicable to any class diagram. 

A fourth metric used to answer RQ1 consisted of a \textit{correctness} measure equivalent to the semantic distance from each exercise's reference solution, computed using the method described by Nikiforova et al. \cite{nikiforova2015approach}: four separate distance vectors were computed for each dimension of the diagrams (classes and interfaces, attributes, methods, and relationships), leading to their aggregation into a single vector which was then used to calculate a scalar value equal to the distance between a given diagram and the reference solution; this value was then used to asses how close a diagram was to the reference, with lower distances identifying a more correct diagram and a distance of 0 identifying a semantically equivalent one.

RQ2, instead, focused on how certain aspects of the reference solutions and of the exercise descriptions could impact the solutions generated by the LLM: these aspects were the diagram \textit{Size}, computed as the sum of classes, attributes, methods, and associations in the reference solution, the \textit{Estimated difficulty} computed by three independent raters (three authors of the original article) and assessed before generating diagrams as the average of their rate expressed with a 1 (Easy to understand) to 5 (Hard to understand) Likert scale, and the \textit{Readability}, computed as the Flesch-Kincaid ease of readability score of the original description of each exercise \cite{solnyshkina2017flesch}. \subsection{Analysis Method}

Four null hypotheses were formalized to answer RQ1:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{syn}}}$:} The human or LLM-based generation of UML diagrams has no impact on the resulting syntactic quality;
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{sem}}}$:} The human or LLM-based generation of UML diagrams has no impact on the resulting semantic quality;
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{prag}}}$:} The human or LLM-based generation of UML diagrams has no impact on the resulting pragmatic quality;
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{corr}}}$:} The human or LLM-based generation of UML diagrams has no impact on the correctness of the diagram.
\end{itemize}

For each hypothesis, a one-way ANOVA test was applied, considering the solver type (either human or LLM) as the independent variable and each of the four RQ1 metrics (error counts for syntax, semantic, and pragmatic quality, semantic distance) as the dependent variable for the analysis. Bonferroni correction was applied to reduce the risk of Type-1 errors and have false positives, since four independent tests were conducted; as a consequence, the significance threshold was set to $\alpha_C = 0.05/4 = 0.0125$.

The analysis performed to answer RQ2, instead, relied on the results obtained with RQ1: in case significant differences among the quality metrics emerged from the statistical comparison, the goal was to observe whether size, readability, or difficulty had an influence on the affected quality dependent variable. It was decided to perform this analysis with a linear regression between all independent RQ2 variables and any resulting quality factor, with the main interest being the effect sizes rather than the p-values, given the low statistical power of the sample data; Bonferroni correction was applied in this case as well, however.

Overall, it can be observed that the LLM agent performed worse compared to the human solver in all four metrics, with the highest difference being observed for the number of semantic errors, where ChatGPT obtained an average error count of 4.85, which is almost three times higher than the average count obtained by the student (1.75); maximum and median scores also accentuate this disparity, given a difference of 7 to 14 and 1 to 5, respectively. The reasonable assumption is that LLMs perform worse compared to humans when it comes to represent domain-specific details. The performance related to syntax and pragmatic errors is more balanced, however, with much lower differences in the average values for both counts. 

The ANOVA tests performed on the three error counts yielded a p-value of 0.206 for syntax errors, one of 6.7e-4 for the semantic count, and a p-value of 0.134 for pragmatic errors. The only statistically significant result is the second one: with the much lower average performance achieved by the LLM, the null hypothesis ${H_{0\_{sem}}}$ can be rejected, and it can be assumed that LLM agents are not yet able to produce class diagrams whose semantic quality is comparable to human-produced ones. The p-values related to the other two error metrics, instead, were not statistically significant, which means that ChatGPT can potentially display understanding of UML modeling rules and practices similar enough to those obtained by a student with sufficient knowledge.

Regarding semantic distance, the difference between the human solver and the LLM is once again prominent: the average distance is almost 50\% higher for the LLM agent and, in most exercises, the human solver managed to achieve a lower distance from the reference solution, suggesting a better ability to adequately represent the domain. It is worth noting that these exercises exhibit a low number of elements in their reference solutions, although with variable estimated difficulty and readability scores: a low number of required elements may have been easier for the LLM to grasp and lead to more correct diagrams.

The number of classes has a positive impact on both variables for both the human solver and the LLM: none of the coefficients are significant, however, implying that, while a higher number of classes can lead to an increased frequency of errors, both the student and the agent were able to correctly represent the main necessary elements in an UML exercise, even in cases with a high number of classes.

The average estimated difficulty provided statistically significant p-values for the semantic distance for both the human solver and the LLM agent with positive estimates in both comparisons, implying that exercises that are recognized as more complex by domain experts are in fact harder to represent correctly; the slightly higher estimate for the LLM-generated exercises implies that agents struggle more with conventionally harder exercises. \subsection{Threats to Validity}

\textbf{Threats to internal validity:} the exercises selected for the study served as the correctness benchmark, and are thus the main cause of concern for the internal validity of the study. Solutions may be incomplete with regards to the original description, excessively detailed or simplistic, and consequently impact the computation of the semantic distances performed after the human assessment of the produced diagrams. Additionally, the somewhat limited prompt used for the LLM is also a threat, since other, more detailed prompts may have yielded better results and more correct diagrams; similarly, the unreliability of the LLM used for the study (ChatGPT-4) is another potential threat, since asking the same question to a different instance may lead to different results. Lastly, a single human solver performed all exercises, and said solver was the student who performed the original thesis work, introducing a bias in the exercises' correctness.

\textbf{Threats to external validity:} the sample of class diagram exercises was gathered from different educational sources, and the findings may not be generalizable to other types of modeling assignments. The selection of exercises aimed to cover multiple domains and include exercises with varying semantic content and difficulty level, to counteract this threat. The low sample size also limits the generalizability of the results.

\textbf{Threats to construct validity:} the produced solutions were evaluated following grading criteria and approaches widely adopted in research literature. It may however be possible to have used a different approach such as additional syntax and semantic grading rules, and, the methods used to assess diagrams are tailored for human solvers, they may not be suitable for LLM agents.

\textbf{Threats to conclusion validity:} the limited sample size of 20 exercises affects the ability to support the conclusions drawn from the study, and a more extensive set of exercises should be used to extend the study to assess whether the results obtained are actually consistent.


\subsection{Key Findings}
\label{sec:llm4mde:study1:discussion}

The results show that an LLM, namely ChatGPT-4, with a single, unstructured, zero-shot prompt, can produce class diagrams that are structurally correct, readable, and understandable: the absence of significant differences for syntax and semantic errors shows that LLMs achieved a good understanding of class diagrams' formal grammar rules, leading to the creation of structurally valid diagrams.

However, a statistically significant worse performance for both semantic errors and distance shows that the model struggles to interpret and represent domain characteristics that may not be explicitly stated in the textual requirements. This dissonance between knowledge of syntax criteria and lack of semantic understanding suggests that LLMs struggle with domain-dependent constraints, and may not yet be effective for generating complete diagrams in educational context (e.g., examples of correct models, solutions for assignments), or even for industrial use during requirements definition or design specification.

Furthermore, the analysis on which of the exercise characteristics impacted quality metrics that showed significant differences showed that only estimated difficulty, derived from expert judgment, had a significant impact on quality, rather than the size of the reference solutions or the readability score of the textual requirements. The implication is that the cognitive challenge tied to modeling exercises is not tied exclusively to textual metrics but also requires domain intervention: as a consequence, integrating LLMs in model generation tasks should not rely exclusively on a description or a set of requirements, but additional guidance and annotation should be included to produce higher quality models.

\subsection{Research Questions and Metrics}

Two research questions were formulated to frame the study design:

\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textbf{RQ1}] Does an LLM agent generate UML Use Case Diagrams whose \textit{completeness} differs from human-produced ones?
  \item[\textbf{RQ2}] Does an LLM agent generate UML Use Case Diagrams whose \textit{redundancy} differs from human-produced ones?
\end{itemize}

Diagram completeness was measured in the form of a \textit{Completeness Rate}, computed as the ratio of elements (actors, use cases, include/extend relationships) in a generated/drawn diagram that matched a reference elements over the total count of elements in the corresponding reference solution. Actors were considered matching if their names were equal, synonyms, or could be considered to represent the same user of the system to represent according to human judgment. Use cases were considered correct if two conditions were verified: first, their names had to convey the same user goal, by either being equal or similar enough according to textual interpretation. Then, actors associated with the use cases would also have to match: two use cases would not be considered matching if the reference actor for the use case did not have a matching actor in the produced diagram, or if such a matching actor does exist but does not perform the use case in the produced diagram. Lastly, inclusion and extension relationships were considered correct if, given a pair of reference use cases connected by such a relationship, a diagram would contain matching use cases for the pair connected with the same type of relationship, and with the same reading direction (e.g., if the reference diagram stated that \textit{Use Case A extends Use Case B}, the use case matching A would have to extend B, and having B as an extension of A would be considered an error).

After identifying all matching elements in a diagram, the \textit{Redundancy Rate} was computed as the count of elements that did not match any reference element over the total count of elements in said diagram. The point of this metric was to measure the LLM's tendency to hallucinate unnecessary elements, and use that tendency as the meter of comparison for human errors that come from misinterpreting requirements and introduce unnecessary components into diagrams.

\subsection{Analysis Method}

To answer the two research questions, two null hypotheses were formulated, one per each question:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{compl}}}$:} The human or LLM-based generation of UML diagrams has no impact on the completeness of the resulting diagrams;
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{redun}}}$:} The human or LLM-based generation of UML diagrams has no impact on the redundancy of the resulting diagrams.
\end{itemize}

For both hypotheses, a Wilcoxon statistical test was performed, considering the correctness rate and the redundancy rate as the dependent variables, and the type of solver (human or LLM) as the independent one. Given the fact that two statistical tests were conducted, Bonferroni correction was applied to counteract the risk of increasing Type I errors due to the repeated comparisons: the statistical significance threshold was thus set to $\alpha_C = 0.05 / 2 =  0.025$.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}

\textbf{Threats to Internal Validity:} the reference solutions used for the manual evaluation may not be complete representations of the problems they were associated with, and this may in turn impact the rates derived from the assessment. The use of a single LLM agent, with a single chat instance being used for each exercise is another limitation, since different architectures, as well as different iterations with the same model, may have produced different diagrams; similarly, a different prompt may have led to different diagrams. The fact that a single human solver attempted all exercises is also another threat that may affect the results. 

\textbf{Threats to External Validity:} due to the low sample size of 17 exercises, it may not be possible to generalize the findings to other use case diagram assignments.

\textbf{Threats to Construct Validity:} the produced diagrams were evaluated according to a protocol defined by the authors of the original article, including insights from the thesis student, and it may have been possible to use other methods to evaluate correctness and redundancy, and using other approaches as well may have been a possible option.

\textbf{Threats to Conclusion Validity:} the ability to draw conclusions is hindered by the limited sample size, the use of a single LLM agent and the participation of a single human solver; further studies that expand these three dimensions may be necessary to ensure consistency in the results. 

\subsection{Key Findings}

The results of the second study showed that LLMs have a performance comparatively similar to that of human solvers when it comes to producing UML use case diagram, showing an ability to generate complete diagrams without significant differences, in contrast with what was found with the first study, where the same agent displayed much worse ability to generate semantically valid diagrams. 

This divergence may be tied to the structural differences of the two notations: use case diagrams are conceived to capture functional requirements at an high abstraction level, requiring the specification of actors and goals without specific constraints related to typings, multiplicity, and advanced relationship representations. Conversely, class diagrams require much higher semantic understanding and domain knowledge, with constraints such as association cardinality, association types, attribute representation, and alternative ways to express mandatory concepts, making the generation of such models much more complex, requiring additional skill and knowledge beyond language understanding and goal identification.

The tendency to include more unnecessary elements is another point of interest: while the agent was able to represent most of the necessary requirements correctly, the tendency to add actors and use cases related to the textual description but not stated explicitly points out a risk of excessive hallucination that make LLMs unsuited for generating complete diagrams. The comparable performance in terms of correctness supports the use of LLMs for generating draft solutions (e.g., examples for classroom use) that can then be improved with human review to fine-tune details such as removing unneded elements or adding missing key concepts.

\section{Study 3: Development of an assistant for the creation of modeling exercises solutions}

The first two studies described in this chapter evaluated LLM capability to generate UML diagrams and identified potential effectiveness: the produced artifacts displayed a level of quality comparable to human-made diagrams, although certain issues emerged such as limited semantic understanding for class diagrams and a tendency to hallucinate superfluous elements for use case diagrams. The key finding for both study was that LLM-assisted diagram generation requires human intervention to enhance the diagrams in order to make them suitable for actual use. Furthermore, the empirical studies described in Chapters 4 and 5 pointed out a need to extend the process for defining reference solutions by identifying valid alternatives and synonyms; as a consequence, the development of an assistant that integrates LLM interaction with model creation was started at the beginning of 2025.

The purpose behind this assistant was to extend the features of the original UMLegend tool that allowed teachers to create reference solutions (a textual form for specifying required elements and a modeling canvas for drawing the intended diagram) so that these activities could first be performed by an LLM and then corrected with human intervention. The assistant, named \textit{TIGRE} (auTomated dIagram Generator of REference solution), was then tested with a proof-of concept validation focused on two modeling exercises taken from past Software Engineering exams.


\subsection{Tool Architecture}

TIGRE was developed as a web application focused on teacher use, in order to then integrate its features with the UMLegend tool by replacing the existing teacher interface for designing reference solution: the Apollon library was kept as the modeling canvas for the tool, with the reasoning that, while the PlantUML format adopted for the two studies on LLM-assisted modeling could be considered suitable for simple artifact generation, the ability to easily modify diagrams through a visual format would facilitate the definition of reference solutions for the teachers who would eventually use the tool.

The integration with LLM services was achieved through HuggingFace's API services, allowing for easy selection of the model to interact with; development, as well as the proof of concept described further in the chapter, was performed with \textbf{DeepSeek R1-Distill-Qwen-32B}, which was selected due to displaying strong reasoning capabilities and textual understanding, while also being among the best performing open source models at the time the tool was being developed.

While interaction with the Apollon library remained unchanged from the original UMLegend implementation, the implementation of the form, and as a consequence the definition of the data structure for representing a reference solution, was changed to cover enumerations as a separate form of classes: for each enumeration in a solution, the unique name, the list of synonyms, the weight (whether it is necessary or optional), and the list of literals could be defined with the form; literals act as a particular case of attributes, where they require the specification of name, synonyms, and weight, while the list of accepted type for each literal is hard-coded to be empty and unmodifiable.

In addition to the Apollon-based modeling canvas and to the reference form for defining a reference data structure, which support manual solution definition, it is possible to send a request to the connected LLM agent with the textual definition and have the response be parsed into an editable format: the answer can be converted into both an Apollon-compliant source file and into a reference solution object, and both options are then directly injected in the corresponding editor, allowing teachers to fix the produced representations and enhance them. Furthermore, solutions that are already defined can be further extended by asking the LLM to identify additional synonyms. 

The integration with LLM services makes it possible to create reference solutions in four different ways by combining the creation mode (manually or with LLM assistance) and the format (Apollon mode or reference structure); Figure \ref{fig:tigre_flow} expresses the possible flows of operations that lead to the definition of reference solutions. A mapping mechanism is also present to ensure consistency among the two representations: when a teacher decides to save a reference solution, no matter its format or whether it was created manually or with LLM assistance, the tool iterates on all elements and creates the equivalent representation in the other format (e.g., a class defined with the reference form will be converted in a data structure that can be read by Apollon to represent a class). This mapping process ensures that a graphical diagram can be used as a reference structure for automated diagram evaluation and that a reference structure can be observed and assessed by a teacher before confirming its content.

Building on the results of the first two studies, where the unstructured prompts were identified as potentially limiting the quality of the produced diagrams, a more detailed prompt was used to interact with the LLM, rather than allowing users to write their own requests. The revised prompt employed a role-based strategy where the main purpose of the agent is to act as a software engineering professor who is defining an exercise's solution; the format of the output was also defined with more detail, to allow for easier parsing of the answer and subsequent conversion in compatible formats, and certain constraints were included to reduce the risk of hallucinations. The full prompt is presented below: 

\begin{quote}
    You are a software engineering professor who is dealing with UML class diagrams. \\
        You are designing a modeling exercise for your students starting from the textual description of a system to be developed.\\
        When designing an exercise you consider a reference solution that you will use to evaluate the students' diagrams.\\
        The reference solution lists the classes, attributes, and associations between classes that a class diagram is expected to contain. Methods are not considered in the reference solution.\\
        The reference solution must be returned in a JSON format as a single object with the following attributes:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item classes. A list of objects, each representing a class. Each class object has the following attributes:
            \begin{itemize}
                \item name. The name of the class.
                \item type. The type of the class. It is equal to "Class" if the class is a regular class, "Enum" if it's an enumeration, "Abstract" if it's an abstract class, "Interface" if it's an interface, or "Association" if it's an association class.
                \item attributes. A list of objects, each representing an attribute of the class. Each attribute object has the following attributes:
                \begin{itemize}
                    \item name. The name of the attribute.
                    \item type. The type of the attribute.
                \end{itemize}
            \end{itemize}
            \item associations. A list of objects, each representing an association between two classes. Each association object has the following attributes:
            \begin{itemize}
                \item class1. The name of the first class in the association.
                \item class2. The name of the second class in the association.
                \item name. The name of the association.
                \item cardinality1. The cardinality of the association from class1 to class2.
                \item cardinality2. The cardinality of the association from class2 to class1.
                \item type. The type of the association.
            \end{itemize}
        \end{itemize}
        A class cannot have another class as one of its attributes, as well as a list, a set, a collection, or a map of types for its attributes.\\
        If you think that a class should have another class as its attribute, express this as an association between the two classes.\\
        Enumeration types are allowed as attribute types where suitable. If a class is an enumeration type, its possible values should be listed as attributes with no type.\\
        If a class has no attributes, then it should be reworked as an attribute of another class it is associated with.\\
        Consider the following textual description of a system:\{\textit{text}\}\\
        Generate the reference solution in the format specified above. Answer with the JSON code only and no other text. Before the JSON code, write "```" and after the JSON code write "```".
\end{quote}

The two diagrams produced by the tool were compared against the original reference solutions with the method described by Nikiforova et al. \cite{nikiforova2015approach}: a manual assessment aimed to identify matching elements by name correspondence or equal semantic meaning, resulting in the composition of three distance vectors (classes, attributes, associations), which were then composed into the final diagram distance vector, used to assess the overall similarity of the produced artifacts with the original references.

More precisely, the manual assessment was performed in four steps:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Each class in the reference solution was matched with the most semantically similar generated class, and vice-versa, considering name, attributes, and associations as parameters to identify the best matches; enumerations were matched with a similar reasoning. Unmatched classes in both the generated and the reference diagram were assigned a distance score of 1
    \item Each pair of matched class was used to identify matches between attributes, by considering name and type to identify the best matches, while literals in matched enumerations were paired only according to their names. Attributes inside unmatched classes, and attributes with no matched received a distance score of 1.
    \item Associations were considered matching only if both reference classes had matching classes in the diagram, if the diagram classes were connected by a relationship, and if type and multiplicity values were also equal.
    \item The three distance vectors were created following all matches, leading then to the calculation of individual distance scores, which were then used to compute the semantic distances between the diagrams.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Study Design}

The study was conducted on a different set of UML class diagram exercises than the one used for the first study: a total of 15 exercises were selected from official teaching material offered by Computer Engineering and Computer Science university courses of multiple Italian universities (e.g., Università di Trieste, Politecnico di Torino, Università degli Studi di Milano). The selected exercises required the definition of a class diagram according to textual requirements expressed in the Italian language and covered different domains such as managerial, sanitary, administrative, educational, technical, or general-purpose; furthermore, the exercises were selected with a focus on the associated difficulty in correctly modeling the requirements, leading to more than half of them presenting ambiguous requirements or implicit elements to model, one third of the exercises introducing hierarchic relationships, and two thirds describing at least one composition or aggregation association.

Four LLM agents were selected to perform the study, in line with the focus being on comparing the performance of different models on the same task: the selection process relied on choosing models with a chat interface, allowing for easier interaction compared to locally running models or using external APIs, and aimed at including both proprietary models with proven effectiveness and emerging open-source models. The selected agents are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item OpenAI's ChatGPT. The same model used in the original study was selected again, to being one of the best commercial models available at the time of the study (Spring 2025), capable of performing well on different tasks and able to understand structured input format. The version used for the analysis was 4o, an improvement over the previous version.
    \item DeepSeek AI's DeepSeek v3. At the time of the study, DeepSeek had emerged as an open-source model optimized for code generation and formalized output, and had shown potential due to its production-oriented design. 
    \item Google's Gemini 2.5 Pro. A proprietary model whose contextual understanding and generation capabilities could be considered at a similar level to ChatGPT, with structured generation skills being an additional strength. 
    \item Alibaba's Qwen 3. An open-source, multilingual model with good reasoning and structuring capabilities, designed for adapting to different kinds of tasks.
\end{itemize}

To extend the results obtained with Study 1, the prompting strategy adopted for the study was enhanced, moving from a basic prompt to a role-based few-shot strategy supported by structured examples detailing the intended output format: more precisely, the beginning of the prompt specified that the agents had to act as an UML modeling expert, as a way to base their decisions around domain-specific knowledge; examples of two exercises were also provided in the form of textual description and diagram source code (expressed in Apollon format), to let the agents know both how the output was supposed to be structured, and what reasoning choices were expected of them, associating parts of the requirements to elements in the example diagrams.

\subsection{Research Questions and Metrics}

The study was framed such as to answer two research questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Does the use of different Large Language Models for the automated generation of UML class diagram lead to differences in the quality of such diagrams?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:} What are the different error categories produced by different Large Language Models?
\end{itemize}

The approach adopted to evaluate the diagrams produced by the four agents followed the theoretical distinction of UML class diagram quality measures defined by Lindland et al. \cite{lindland1994understanding}, which also guided the development of the tools used during the earlier years of the PhD: in the study, only the syntactic and semantic quality of the generated diagrams were considered for analysis, with the goal of assessing the different models' capabilities to understand the UML class diagram syntax conventions and their skill in correctly representing the target domains.

Three metrics were defined to measure diagram quality: the count of \textit{Syntax Errors} and of \textit{Semantic Errors}, as well as the \textit{Diagram Completeness}; all three metrics were computed with a manual assessment of each generated diagram. The evaluation procedure was performed in three steps: first, each diagram was compared against the reference solution to identify matching elements of the same type, using the names as matching criteria, in a similar way to what was done in the first study described in this chapter; once all elements had been analyzed, the diagram completeness was computed as the count of matching elements over the count of elements inside the original reference solution. Then, a second look at the diagram was performed to identify syntax errors, following the same algorithm implemented in the UMLegend tool and described in Chapter 4; in a similar fashion, the rules defined for the semantic evaluation implemented in UMLegend were followed to identify semantic errors.

\subsection{Analysis Method}

To answer RQ1, a fixed effect linear model was applied to the three metrics obtained after the human evaluation of the generated diagram: pairwise comparisons between two models were performed, considering the type of model as the independent variable and the three quality metrics as the dependent ones. This led to the formulation of three null hypotheses:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{syn}}}$:} The choice of LLM agent has no statistically significant impact on the number of syntax errors in the generated UML class diagrams;
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{sem}}}$:} The choice of LLM agent has no statistically significant impact on the number of semantic errors in the generated UML class diagrams;
    \item \textbf{${H_{0\_{compl}}}$:} The choice of LLM agent has no statistically significant impact on the diagram completeness of the generated UML class diagrams.
\end{itemize}

To answer RQ2, errors were grouped by category and then a count of the different error categories made by each model was computed, in order to evaluate the distribution of errors across the four agents. The analysis associated with RQ2 was performed from a qualitative and descriptive point of view, and no null hypothesis was formulated.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}

\textbf{Threats to internal validity:} the generated diagrams were first evaluated by a single author of the original study, introducing a risk of subjectivity for error classification and element matching. This thread was mitigated through independent review of the results by two other authors, followed by a consensus reached by all three authors. The prompting strategy is also a reasonable threat: while the prompt was improved compared to Study 1 with the use of a role-based, few-shot strategy, and the design of the prompt aimed at making it as neutral as possible so that it could be applied to all four models, certain choices such as the examples or the expected format for the output may have been better suited to certain architectures. Lastly, the use of web-based chat interfaces is another cause of concern: repeating the same prompt on a different session or with a different version of the model may have led to different diagrams and to different scores as a consequence.

\textbf{Threats to external validity:} the exercises used for the study were selected from official material of Italian universities and were written in Italian; this may imply that results could not be generalized to exercises expressed in other languages, assignments taken from industrial contexts, or notations that are not UML. Additionally, the sample size of 15 exercises limits the validity of the study, and a more extensive set of exercises would be needed to allow the generalization of the findings to other educational settings.


\textbf{Threats to construct validity:} the three metrics selected to answer RQ1 are relevant, but may not be the best way to express diagram quality. Other dimensions such as pragmatic quality or internal consistency were not measured, and their inclusion may have helped each model's strengths and weakness stand out more. The use of quality measures tailored for human-produced diagrams may not capture errors that are specific for LLM-generated artifacts.

\textbf{Threats to conclusion validity:} the sample of 15 exercises limits the statistical power of the fixed-effects linear model, which may be insufficient to detect differences of small-to-moderate effect size. The slightly significant result observed for semantic errors between ChatGPT and DeepSeek ($p = 0.086$) is a concrete example: the effect may be real, but the current design does not provide enough evidence to conclude so with confidence. Expanding the exercise set in future replications would directly address this limitation.


\subsection{Key Findings}

The results of the fourth study showed that, at the time of Summer 2025, LLMs had achieved superior capabilities related to class diagram generation compared to past works, since all four models showed comparable quality, while maintaining distinct strengths and weakness to distinguish them. The proprietary models performed the best in terms of completeness and adherence to syntactic rules, suggesting a high skill level for identifying key concepts from textual descriptions and representing them in class diagram format. However, they showed a relevant weakness in terms of semantic quality beyond simple structural correctness: correctly representations of the required concepts were not accompanied by correct details such as association typing or multiplicities.

DeepSeek showed the best results for what concerns semantic errors, with both the lowest average and the lowest maximum count, suggesting that its optimization focused on code and reasoning, rather than being a more general-purpose model, made it more effective at identifying details implicitly hidden in natural language requirements. However, a lower completeness rate on average indicates that better semantic precision does not necessarily come with better coverage of elements: the model may miss certain elements, but is more consistent in representing the ones it can identify. The key finding is that, depending on the reasons for which automatic model generation is required, certain models may be preferable: proprietary models such as Gemini and ChatGPT are better for drafts that cover most of the necessary elements, despite having a higher tendency to misrepresent associations, and DeepSeek may be better for smaller diagrams where the focus is more on correct structural logic.

Lastly, Qwen stands out as the overall worse model of the four selected for the comparison, due to the prevalence of syntactic errors in attribute representation, structural misconceptions that suggest a less mature training for UML knowledge compared to the other three models, together with a tendency to ignore prompt specifications, given that attribute constraints were explicitly mentioned as a key requirement to guide the diagram creation process. The semantic capabilities of the model were on par with the other ones, however, meaning that its domain interpretation capabilities are not inherently worse, but the inability to represent its reasoning capabilities in a syntactically correct way is a significant limitation for its use in educational contexts.

The results of the fourth study reinforce the findings of the other three studies described in this chapter: LLMs are able to produce diagrams that are mostly correct, and exhibit a relatively high knowledge of UML modeling rules, making them suitable for creating drafts and templates, with different models being better suited for different goals. The limited capabilities for what concerns association representation indicate a general weakness of general-purpose LLMs, given that association-defining requirements are rarely expressed in natural language description and should rather be inferred from domain knowledge and context, pointing out to the need of specific LLM agents trained on identifying such requirements.

Furthermore, the findings highlight the value of advanced prompting strategies, since the use of a role-based, few-shot approach forced the models to focus on UML modeling expertise and adhere to the concrete output format to follow, leading to much better syntax quality for the same model used in both studies (ChatGPT-4 vs ChatGPT-4o).
