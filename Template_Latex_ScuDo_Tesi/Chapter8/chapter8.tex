\chapter{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{ch7}

\graphicspath{{Chapter8/}}

This PhD thesis aimed to address the gap identified back in Chapter \ref{ch1}: the importance of software modeling in the development process is not reflected in educational curricula due to learning the necessary notations being seen as a complex hurdle and requiring continuous and personalized feedback loops that are not feasible with standard teaching approaches and are rarely covered by educational tools. Gamification was identified as a potentially effective strategy to address this gap, with gamified modeling education also being a relatively underexplored area particularly lacking in rigorous, longitudinal empirical evaluations; as a consequence, the research activities undertaken in the first years of the PhD aimed at addressing this gap with the development of gamified tools for modeling education. These research activities had the goal of answering questions related to the effectiveness of game mechanics in improving student performance, leading to learning benefits over long periods, and being designed in a way that students can appreciate.

A second set of research activities, born from both the limitations emerged from the early empirical studies and from the observations derived from related literature, focused on the large language models that were becoming increasingly popular in late 2023 and on their potential applications to model-driven tasks, leading to the formulation of questions related to the agents' ability to generate diagrams comparable to human solvers, reduce teacher cognitive effort in designing solutions, and compare diagram generation potential across architectures and prompting strategies. A total of six research questions were formulated, three per each set of thematic research activities, and answering these questions make it possible to state the PhD work's findings, its limitations, the lessons learned, and what needs to be done next.

\textbf{RQ1} asked whether gamification can bring measurable improvements to student performance when applied in single-session modeling activities. The two controlled experiments described in Chapters \ref{ch3} and \ref{ch4} found that this is mostly true: a finding common to both studies was that, when interacting with a gamified tool, students were more inclined to have their diagrams assessed by the automated evaluators, suggesting increased commitment for solving exercises; this, in turn, led to performance improvements such as a significant improvement in semantic correctness for the BIPMIN study, and a significant reduction of syntactic and semantic errors and a significant increase in diagram completeness for UML class diagram exercises solved with UMLegend. However, gamification never led students to make significant variations in their diagrams, measured as changes in their size: the interpretation is that gamification can guide students in a refining process rather than an expanding one; students incrementally corrected and improved the existing elements thanks to the guidance given by the gamified feedback loop. In single session usage, gamification heightens the students' attention to correctness rather than broadens it.

\textbf{RQ2} focused on assessing whether longitudinal interaction with a gamified modeling tool leads to measurable improvements in student performance observed in the final course evaluations, with the longitudinal study described in Chapter \ref{ch5} measuring this through a comparison with a cohort of students who followed the traditional learning approach. The findings related to RQ2 can be considered the most significant finding of the gamified-focused research activities, since students who used BIPMIN throughout the course to learn BPMN fundamentals achieved statistically significantly higher grades on BPMN activities for both the course's final exam and the group project assignment, showing that gamification can support learning of basic BPMN best practices as well as skills that translate to being able to handle more complex requirements. Student performance over time was also observed, although with slightly less positive results: while a reduction of semantic errors could be observed when moving from the first exercise to the last, suggesting improvements compared to the beginning of the course and the ability to internalize correct domain representation constraints over time, completeness improvements were not as prominent, with the exercise ordering being affected by a difficulty mismatch that placed a more complex exercise before its optimal scheduling, in relation to the students' obtained skills.

\textbf{RQ3} aimed at measuring the students' overall perception of gamified modeling education, while also gathering insights on which game mechanics are perceived as the most supportive in the learning process. The consistent findings across all three empirical studies point toward mechanics that introduce direct, element-specific corrective guidance are the most effective and appreciated: regardless of the study length (longitudinal or single-session), mechanics such as diagram coloring, completeness indicators, and lists for errors and matched elements were the most appreciated, suggesting that the research work performed over the PhD addressed the goal established at first (that is, the development of effective tools that provide actionable feedback on large scale); conversely, mechanics intended for long term were among the less appreciated in all studies, including the longitudinal one where the expectation was to see them rise in popularity. The interpretation for this result relies on Self Determination Theory \cite{deci1985intrinsic, deci2000intrinsic}: the need for competence is covered with the appreciated feedback mechanics, and relatedness and autonomy needs covered with social and cosmetic elements (leaderboards, avatar customization) require different social interaction mechanics other than pure competition. Another positive finding related to RQ3 was an improvement, for what concerns the redesigned BIPMIN tool, in the overall perception of the gamified experience, with enjoyment and perceived creative freedom being seen in a much better light compared to the prototype version, confirming the effectiveness of the new game mechanics framework.

Moving from the gamified-focused research activities to the LLM-based ones, \textbf{RQ4} introduced large language models as a topic in the PhD by asking whether they are comparable to humans when it comes to UML diagram generation, finding that the results vary depending on the notation. GPT-4 was proven to be significantly worse than a competing human solver in terms of class diagram semantic error count and semantic distance to references, while being comparable in terms of syntax and pragmatic quality; no statistically significant difference was instead observed for use case diagrams when comparing them in terms of completeness or redundancy (unnecessary/hallucinated elements). The reason behind this divergence can be explained with the structural differences between the two notations: use case diagrams rely on the identification of actors and functional goals from textual description, a task that LLMs can perform with relative ease, while class diagrams require advanced text understanding capabilities and domain knowledge to derive association types, multiplicity values, or distinguish between classes and attributes. LLMs may thus be used as viable generators for use case diagram drafts, but the quality of their class diagram output makes them more suitable for the production of draft diagrams, or as part of an integrated process that involves human refinement.

\textbf{RQ5} continued the research thread by addressing the practical dimension of LLM capability through the development of a prototype intelligent assistant for the creation of reference solution \cite{garaccione2026automated}. The proof of concept on the TIGRE tool demonstrated that the use of a role-based prompt can generate structurally valid draft solutions in less than a minute, capturing most required classes with ease while at the same time exhibiting the weaknesses exposed in the first two studies. LLM assistance moves instructor effort on the semantic details that agents struggle with, while streamlining the structural definition process; controlled comparative studies will be necessary to assess whether this constitutes a net time saving compared to manual diagram creation.

Lastly, \textbf{RQ6} compared the performance of different architectures with advanced prompting strategies, finding out that proprietary models (ChatGPT, Gemini) had the best performance on completeness and syntactic quality, producing no errors, while the code-oriented DeepSeek proved the best in terms of semantic adherence \cite{garaccione2025models}. The key finding is that the shift from unstructured zero-shot prompts to advanced techniques that provide input and output examples and apply role based strategies can significantly improve performance, given that ChatGPT went from the average syntax error count of 0.9 to performing zero such errors, confirming that prompt engineering can eliminate the rare syntactic issues, while at the same time not being sufficient to cover the semantic gap.

The contribution this thesis brings to the state of the art can be evaluated across three axes that emerged from the background review. For what concerns gamified tools applied to software modeling education (Section \ref{sec:back_gamif_mde}), the thesis consists of the first empirical demonstration that a free-form, gamified modeling environment can improve diagram quality in both single exercises and longitudinal deployments, while also providing the first evidence that exposure to a gamified tool for an entire semester brings benefits to academic performance, findings no existing previous tool had yet reached. The automated diagram evaluation aspect (Section \ref{sec:uml_eval}) was covered by the three-phase evaluation pipelines developed within UMLegend and the revised version of BIPMIN, advancing the state of the art by combining syntactic, semantic and pragmatic assessment with a gamified feedback loop offering element-specific error messaging and correctness indicators, extending the aggregate scores used in UMLGrade \cite{farias2020whats} and the scalar distance computation defined by Nikiforova et al. \cite{nikiforova2015approach}. Lastly, the four studies focused on LLMs in software modeling practices provide advancements beyond single-model and single-notation assessments \cite{camara2023assessment, chen2023automated} thanks to benchmarking evaluations that cover two notations and multiple LLM agents, providing guidelines for model selection tailored around educational diagram generation. 

The complete research programs identified three relevant lessons that no individual research question can completely cover. First of all, feedback quality is of the utmost importance, given the preference for assisting mechanics over competitive or cosmetic ones in all three empirical studies, implying that gamification in modeling education functions primarily as part of an automated tutoring layer rather than a motivational addition, and tool design should focus on that aspect. Secondly, the quality of evaluation systems set a ceiling for what gamification studies can measure: failures depending on incomplete synonym identification caused false negatives, leading to inflated error counts and reduced completeness scores. As a consequence, the positive effects reported across the empirical studies should be considered lower bounds instead of accurate estimates of the educational benefits. Lastly, the LLM studies and the gamification experiments converge on the same bottleneck from opposite directions: the evaluation engines are not able to completely assess semantic equivalence, and LLMs cannot reliably generate diagrams that respect said equivalence, pointing to an open problem connecting both research areas of the PhD.

The limitations of the PhD research impact the conclusions drawn in this chapter, and relate to aspects observed in the experiments' design choices. First of all, the empirical tool evaluations were all conducted at Politecnico di Torino in Masters courses, meaning that results may not transfer equally to undergraduate programs, professional environments, or even institutions that follow different curricular structures; furthermore, student populations were assessed, for what concerns the single-session studies, at a point where modeling competence was already established, and this competence reflected on near-null impact in terms of syntactic error reduction, pointing to a ceiling effect rather than a limit of the approach, given that no studies were performed specifically at the beginning of modeling courses, leaving an important research area underexplored. 

LLM studies all shared the same limitations, with constraints related to small sample sizes, involving a single human solver as a baseline or as the manual evaluator, or using interactive chat interfaces instead of controlled methods such as API interactions or running the models locally; furthermore, the use of the Italian language for certain exercises may not allow generalization of the findings, and the rapidly evolving landscape of LLM agents means that the performance observed in the three empirical studies should be considered a baseline measurement instead of a fixed, immutable characterization.

The planned future research agenda builds from certain key findings and from the student opinions gathered with the gamified studies. The most important direction is the integration of the BIPMIN and UMLegend tools into a single educational platform, allowing for the observation of long-term mechanics' effects, given that they scored less favorably in the latter tool's empirical evaluation. The aim is to develop a complete platform that can support all modeling notations covered within a course, to ensure no gap is found within student perception and allowing complete gamification of the course aspects; for this purpose, the UMLegend tool has been extended at the tail end of the PhD work to cover UML use case diagrams as another notation \cite{garaccione2026enhancinggamifiedtooluml}, with a more modular implementation approach that will allow porting BIPMIN functionalities into a complete educational tool. The improved version is currently deployed as the modeling environment used in the 2026 edition of the Software Engineering course, with plans to analyze its impact on student performance once the course is over.

The limitations related to the evaluation systems imply the necessity of LLM-based semantic matching components to enhance, and potentially replace, normalized edit-distance comparison: development of preliminary architectures that use local LLM agents to evaluate Apollon diagrams using the reference solution structure defined by the combined TIGRE and UMLegend tools is currently underway, with empirical tests on their capabilities being planned for Autumn 2026.

Lastly, given the focus of the PhD work on developing approaches to provide large scale feedback for educational modeling activities, a controlled study that imagines LLM architectures as modeling assistants in university courses is currently being planned for the 2026-2027 edition of the Information Systems course: the aim of this study is to build on the findings from the studies described in Chapter \ref{ch6} and assess the quality of LLM-assisted modeling in comparison to the regular approach; the low semantic capabilities of LLMs could be used as a way for students to identify issues and how to correct them.
